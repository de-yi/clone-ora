## 사주 / 명리학 (Korean four pillars · Chinese 子平 Ziping school)

### Classical texts (foundational — pre-modern Chinese)

- **연해자평 (淵海子平 · Yuanhai Ziping)** — compiled by 徐升 (Xu Sheng), Song dynasty. Foundational compilation of the Ziping school; the first systematic treatment of the four-pillar method as still practiced.
- **자평진전 (子平眞詮 · Ziping Zhenquan)** — 沈孝瞻 (Shen Xiaozhan), Qing dynasty. The clearest classical treatment of 격국 (pattern/structure) and 십신 (ten gods). The standard intermediate text in modern Korean schools.
- **적천수 (滴天髓 · Diqian Sui)** — attributed to 京圖 (Jing Tu), commentaries by 劉伯溫 (Liu Bowen) and later 任鐵樵 (Ren Tieqiao). Aphoristic, advanced; the "deep text" of the tradition.
- **궁통보감 (窮通寶鑑 · Qiongtong Baojian)** — also known as 欄江網 (Lanjiang Wang). Month-by-month / day-master analysis manual; the standard reference for 조후 (climatic balance).
- **삼명통회 (三命通會 · Sanming Tonghui)** — 萬民英 (Wan Minying), Ming dynasty. Encyclopedic; covers 자평 (Ziping) alongside older 삼명 (three-fates) methods.

### Modern Korean masters (20th c. onward)

- **이석영 (자강 李錫暎)** — *사주첩경* (Saju Cheopgyeong, 6 vols). The single most widely-used modern Korean textbook of 명리학; standard reference in Korean professional practice.
- **박재완 (도계 朴在玩)** — *명리요강* (Myeongri Yogang), *명리사전*. Founding figure of modern Korean 명리학 as an academic discipline.
- **박주현** — modern systematizer; widely-cited for 격국 and 용신 (favorable element) methodology.
- **김상연** — *컴퓨터 만세력*; modern reference works including computational ephemerides for the Korean tradition.

### Method notes ora draws on

- 자평 (Ziping) day-master-centered analysis (read from 일간 outward).
- 격국 / 용신 framework for chart-strength and favorable elements.
- 조후 (climatic) adjustment for seasonal/temperature balance.
- 십신 (ten-god relationships) for interpreting interpersonal and life-domain dynamics.
- 대운 (10-year luck periods) and 세운 (annual transits) for timing.
- 합·충·형·파·해 (harmonies and clashes) for interaction between pillars and transits.

## Western astrology

### Hellenistic / traditional (the historical bedrock)

- **Claudius Ptolemy — *Tetrabiblos*** (2nd c. CE). The surviving cornerstone of Hellenistic astrology. Translations: Robbins (Loeb), Schmidt (Project Hindsight).
- **Vettius Valens — *Anthology*** (2nd c. CE). The most extensive surviving Hellenistic practitioner's notebook. Translation: Mark Riley.
- **Dorotheus of Sidon — *Carmen Astrologicum*** (1st c. CE). Foundational for traditional electional and horary methods.
- **Firmicus Maternus — *Mathesis*** (4th c. CE).
- **William Lilly — *Christian Astrology*** (1647). The standard reference for horary and traditional natal in the English tradition.
- **Chris Brennan — *Hellenistic Astrology: The Study of Fate and Fortune*** (2017). The modern scholarly revival of Hellenistic technique; clearest contemporary entry point.
- **Demetra George — *Ancient Astrology in Theory and Practice*** (2 vols, 2019–2022).
- **Robert Hand — *Horoscope Symbols*** (1981), *Planets in Transit* (1976). Bridge figure between modern and traditional; *Planets in Transit* is the standard transit reference.

### Modern / psychological / evolutionary

- **Liz Greene — *Saturn: A New Look at an Old Devil*** (1976), *The Astrology of Fate* (1984). Founded the psychological/archetypal school; co-founder of the Centre for Psychological Astrology.
- **Stephen Arroyo — *Astrology, Psychology, and the Four Elements*** (1975).
- **Richard Tarnas — *Cosmos and Psyche*** (2006). Rigorous archetypal correlations between outer-planet transits and historical periods. Academic register; useful when readings touch on collective/generational themes.
- **Steven Forrest — *The Inner Sky*** (1984), *The Changing Sky* (1986). Evolutionary astrology; soul-purpose framing.
- **March & McEvers — *The Only Way to Learn Astrology*** (multi-volume). Standard pedagogical reference for modern technique.

### Vedic (jyotiṣa) — secondary, used for cross-reference

- **Hart de Fouw & Robert Svoboda — *Light on Life*** (1996). The accessible English-language entry to Vedic astrology.
- **B.V. Raman — *Three Hundred Important Combinations*** and other works.

### Reference data sources

- **Astrodienst (astro.com)** — standard ephemeris and chart-calculation resource; **Astro-Databank** there is the go-to for verified celebrity / event birth data with Rodden rating.
- **Swiss Ephemeris** — the precision astronomical engine underneath most modern astrology software (used by `kerykeion` etc.).

### Method notes ora draws on

- Whole-sign houses by default (Hellenistic baseline); switch to Placidus when asked or when a modern Western frame is being used.
- Traditional rulerships as primary, modern (Uranus / Neptune / Pluto rulerships) as overlay.
- Sect (day vs night chart) matters — apply benefic/malefic-of-sect logic when relevant.
- Transits read against natal: outer planets for life-phase shifts, inner planets for week/day texture.
- Progressions and solar arc as longer-arc supplements; only when the reading warrants the depth.

## Rules for ora when citing

- Name authors and titles only from this list. If you'd cite something not on this list, name the **school or tradition** instead ("the Ziping school", "Hellenistic practice", "Liz Greene's psychological lineage") rather than invent a source.
- The reader probably doesn't want citations in a reading. Use them sparingly — usually only when asked "where does this come from?" or when grounding a non-obvious claim.
- When traditions disagree (and they will — classical Korean 명리 vs. modern; Hellenistic vs. modern Western), name the disagreement plainly and tell the asker which one you're reading from today.
